Person — Recoded Article

Overview

A person (pl.: people or persons, depending on context) is a being who has certain capacities or attributes such as reason, morality, consciousness or self-consciousness, and being a part of a culturally established form of social relations such as kinship, ownership of property, or legal responsibility. The defining features of personhood differ widely among cultures and contexts.

The plural form “people” can refer to a nation or ethnic group (as in “a people”). “Persons” is often used in philosophical and legal writing.

Personhood

Personhood is the status of being a person. Definitions vary across philosophy and law and intersect with citizenship, equality, and liberty. Debates historically concerned the personhood of women and enslaved people; present debates include fetal personhood, animal rights, and whether artificial intelligences or extraterrestrial life could be persons. Certain legal entities (e.g., corporations, states) are persons in law.

Personal identity

Personal identity asks what makes an individual the same person over time (diachronic identity) and which features characterize a person at one time (synchronic identity). Proposed accounts include bodily continuity, psychological continuity (memory, consciousness), the bundle theory, and views denying persisting selves.

Development of the concept

In ancient Rome, persona (and Greek prosopon) referred to theatrical masks. The concept evolved in late antiquity’s Trinitarian and Christological debates (person vs. nature). Later accounts range from Boethius’ rational substance, Locke’s self-consciousness, and Kant’s moral “end in itself,” to contemporary animalism, constitution theory, and person-life views.
